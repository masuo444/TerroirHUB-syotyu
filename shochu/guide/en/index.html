Foundation

What Is Shochu?

Shochu (焼酎 — literally "burnt liquor") is Japan's most consumed spirit by volume, yet remains one of the world's least-known fine spirits outside Asia. It is a distilled beverage produced primarily in the southern island of Kyushu and the Ryukyu archipelago (Okinawa), using local base ingredients — most commonly sweet potato, barley, or rice — that are fermented with koji mold and water, then single-distilled in a pot still to produce honkaku shochu (本格焼酎), or "authentic shochu."

Unlike sake, which is brewed (fermented like beer or wine), shochu is distilled — a process that concentrates alcohol and removes sugars, resulting in a spirit typically bottled at 25% ABV with zero carbohydrates. Unlike vodka or whisky, honkaku shochu is single-distilled to preserve the character of its base ingredient and the terroir of its region. Each sip of imo shochu carries the volcanic soils of Kagoshima; each pour of Okinawan awamori reflects 600 years of Ryukyuan trade culture.

Shochu is also singular in its use of koji (Aspergillus mold) — a microorganism that enzymatically converts starches to fermentable sugars. The three koji varieties — black (kuro), white (shiro), and yellow (ki) — each produce a profoundly different flavor profile, and their selection is one of the master distiller's most consequential decisions. Black koji, original to Okinawa, produces a bold, citric fermentation; white koji (a 1920s mutation of black) gives a smoother, more approachable spirit; yellow koji, borrowed from sake brewing, creates delicate, aromatic expressions.

Honkaku Shochu

Single-distilled in a pot still from a single base ingredient. Also called otsu-rui (乙類). Preserves the natural flavor of sweet potato, barley, rice, brown sugar, or buckwheat. This is what connoisseurs mean when they say "shochu." Typically 25% ABV, but some genshi (undiluted) expressions reach 37–44%.

Korui Shochu

Continuously distilled to near-neutral purity. Also called ko-rui (甲類). Similar in character to vodka — clean, odorless, high-proof. Primarily used as a mixer base for chuhai (shochu highball) cocktails and commercially blended beverages. Not what this guide is about.

Awamori

Okinawa's distinct distilled spirit — legally classified as honkaku shochu but representing its own 600-year tradition. Uses Thai indica (long-grain) rice, exclusively black koji, and an all-koji fermentation unique in the world. Aged expressions (kusu, 3+ years) are among Japan's most complex spirits.

Blended (Konwa)

A legally defined blend of honkaku and korui shochu. Must contain at least 1% honkaku shochu. Common in commercial canned products. Delivers approachability and lower cost at the expense of the terroir character that defines honkaku.

Geographical Indication

Four WTO-Protected Shochu Regions

Japan has secured WTO TRIPS Agreement protection for four shochu and awamori Geographical Indications (GIs) — a legal framework equivalent to Champagne's AOC status in France. These designations guarantee that bottles carrying the GI name were produced in the specified region, from specified ingredients, using traditional methods.

Each GI zone enforces its own production specifications: GI Satsuma requires Kagoshima-grown sweet potato and Kagoshima water; GI Kuma mandates rice grown in the Kuma basin; GI Iki specifies a 1:2 ratio of rice koji to barley, a ratio that defines the spirit's distinctive richness; GI Ryukyu requires Okinawan water, Thai indica rice, and Aspergillus luchuensis (black koji). No other Japanese spirit categories have received comparable international legal protection.
