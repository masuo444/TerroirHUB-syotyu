Terms A–Z

A B C D G H I J K M O R S T W Y Z
A
Amami Shochu奄美焼酎Legal

A general term for shochu produced in the Amami Islands. Specifically refers to kokuto (brown sugar) shochu, which can only be produced in the five inhabited islands of the Amami archipelago under a special provision of Japan's Liquor Tax Act. Kokuto shochu must use rice koji as a mandatory component.

Awamori泡盛Awamori

Okinawa's distinct distilled spirit, produced exclusively in Okinawa Prefecture using Thai indica (long-grain) rice, black koji (Aspergillus luchuensis), and an all-koji fermentation method. Legally classified as honkaku shochu but representing a separate 600-year tradition rooted in Ryukyuan culture. GI Ryukyu protects the category under WTO law. Aged expressions (3+ years) are called kusu (古酒).

Aspergillus luchuensisProduction

The scientific name for black koji mold — the original koji strain used in Okinawa and historically throughout Kyushu. Produces abundant citric acid during fermentation, providing strong protection against spoilage bacteria. Also known as Aspergillus awamori in older taxonomic classifications. The foundation of all awamori production and traditional imo shochu.

Atmospheric distillation常圧蒸留Jonatsu jouryuuProduction

Distillation at normal atmospheric pressure, boiling the moromi at approximately 85°C. Preserves the full spectrum of aromatic compounds from the base ingredient and fermentation, producing bold, complex, richly flavored spirits. Traditional method, preferred by artisan producers. Contrasts with reduced-pressure (gennatsu) distillation.

B
Black koji黒麹Kuro kojiProduction

Aspergillus luchuensis — the original and most traditional koji strain used in shochu and awamori production. Produces abundant citric acid, providing strong protection against spoilage in warm climates. Creates bold, complex spirits with characteristic tartness and depth. Mandatory for all awamori production. Used by artisan imo shochu producers for traditional character.

Bottle keepボトルキープBotoru kiipuCulture

The Japanese izakaya practice of purchasing a whole bottle of shochu, drinking a portion, and leaving the remainder stored at the bar with your name on it for future visits. Economical for regulars, creates personal relationships with the establishment. Standard storage period is 1–3 months. Uniquely Japanese drinking culture practice.

C
ChuhaiチューハイServing

Short for "shochu highball" (shochu + highball). A carbonated mixed drink typically made with korui (neutral) shochu or honkaku shochu combined with sparkling water and a fruit flavoring — most commonly lemon, grapefruit, or ume (plum). One of Japan's most popular RTD (ready-to-drink) alcoholic beverages. Artisan-style chuhai made with honkaku shochu and fresh citrus juice is increasingly available at izakayas.

D
Distillery蒸留所Jouryuusho

A facility that produces distilled spirits. For shochu, the term "shuzo" (酒造) or "honten" (本店) is often used for traditional distilleries. Japan has approximately 970 active honkaku shochu and awamori distilleries, concentrated in Kyushu (Kagoshima, Kumamoto, Oita, Miyazaki, Nagasaki) and Okinawa.

Double fermentation並行複発酵Heiko fukuhakkoProduction

A fermentation method in which saccharification (conversion of starch to sugar by koji) and fermentation (conversion of sugar to alcohol by yeast) occur simultaneously in the same vessel. This "parallel double fermentation" is unique to Japanese brewing and distilling with koji, and is what allows the production of alcohol from non-fruit starchy ingredients without pre-conversion.

G
Genshi原酒Gen-shiProduction

Undiluted shochu, bottled at cask strength — typically 37–44% ABV — without the addition of water used to bring standard shochu to 25% ABV. Genshi expressions are significantly more intense and flavor-concentrated than standard releases, representing the distiller's vision without compromise. Best enjoyed neat with optional small addition of mineral water.

GI Iki壱岐Legal / GI

Geographical Indication protecting barley shochu produced on Iki Island, Nagasaki Prefecture. WTO TRIPS Agreement designation, awarded 1995. Production specifications require the use of barley and rice koji in a mandated 2:1 ratio (barley:rice koji), Iki Island water, and traditional pot still distillation. The rice koji component is what distinguishes GI Iki from typical barley shochu, adding richness and aromatic complexity.

GI Kuma球磨Legal / GI

Geographical Indication protecting rice shochu from the Kuma region of Kumamoto Prefecture — specifically the Kuma River basin and Hitoyoshi city. WTO TRIPS Agreement designation, awarded 1995. Requirements include use of rice grown in the Kuma region, Kuma basin water, pot still distillation, and production within the designated area. Home to 27 distilleries producing some of Japan's most delicate and complex rice spirits.

GI Ryukyu琉球Legal / GI

Geographical Indication protecting awamori from Okinawa Prefecture. WTO TRIPS Agreement designation, awarded 1995. Requirements include use of Thai indica (long-grain) rice, Okinawan water, Aspergillus luchuensis (black koji), all-koji fermentation method, and production within Okinawa Prefecture. The most geographically specific of Japan's four shochu/awamori GIs.

GI Satsuma薩摩Legal / GI

Geographical Indication protecting sweet potato shochu from Kagoshima Prefecture (excluding the Amami Islands). WTO TRIPS Agreement designation, awarded 2005 — the most recently designated of Japan's four shochu GIs. Requirements include Kagoshima-grown sweet potato, Kagoshima water sources, pot still distillation, and production within Kagoshima. Covers approximately 120 distilleries.

H
Honkaku Shochu本格焼酎Honkaku shochuClassification

Literally "authentic shochu." The legally defined category of shochu that is single-distilled in a pot still from a fermented mash of a single base ingredient. Also classified as otsu-rui (乙類) under Japan's Liquor Tax Act. Distinguished from korui shochu (continuously distilled, near-neutral) and konwa shochu (blended). The category that encompasses all artisan, craft, and traditional Japanese distilled spirits — imo, mugi, kome, kokuto, soba — and awamori.

I
Imo芋Ee-moIngredient

Sweet potato — the base ingredient of imo shochu, Japan's most iconic honkaku category. The primary cultivar used is Kogane Sengan (黄金千貫), grown in the volcanic soils of Kagoshima and Miyazaki. Other varieties include Murasaki Masari (purple sweet potato, floral and fruity), Joy White (light, citrus-tinged), and Beni Azuma (stone fruit notes). Sweet potatoes must be processed within 24 hours of harvest due to rapid deterioration.

Imo Shochu芋焼酎Type

Honkaku shochu distilled from sweet potato (imo). Japan's most produced honkaku category by volume. Primary producing regions: Kagoshima (~120 distilleries, GI Satsuma) and Miyazaki (~45 distilleries). Known for its earthy, sweet, full-bodied character that blooms in oyuwari (hot water serving). Black and white koji both used; black koji produces bolder, more traditional expressions; white koji yields smoother, rounder character.

Izakaya居酒屋Ee-zah-kah-yahCulture

Japanese gastropub where shochu is most commonly consumed. Izakayas typically offer extensive shochu menus organized by type (imo, mugi, kome) and region, serving it in various styles (oyuwari, rocks, sodawari). Bottle keep services, all-you-can-drink courses (nomihodai), and knowledgeable staff make izakayas the ideal introduction to shochu culture for newcomers.

J
Jonatsu jouryuu常圧蒸留Production

Atmospheric (normal-pressure) distillation — see "Atmospheric distillation." Produces bold, complex, full-flavored spirits with the character of the base ingredient preserved. The traditional method, preferred by artisan producers for imo, kome, and GI Iki mugi shochu production.

K
Kakutsubo甕壷Ka-ku-tsu-boProduction

Traditional ceramic storage jar used for fermenting and aging shochu. Made from unglazed terracotta, kakutsubo jars allow slow micro-oxygenation through the porous walls, softening the spirit and imparting subtle minerality without adding flavor. Traditional storage vessels for artisan imo and kome shochu. Distinct from the clay kame used for awamori kusu aging in Okinawa.

Kame甕Ka-mehProduction

Traditional Okinawan clay storage vessel used for awamori aging, particularly for kusu (aged awamori). Large, rounded clay jars — traditionally decorated with shisa (Okinawan lion-dog guardian) figures — allow micro-oxygenation and contribute subtle clay minerality to the aging spirit. Kame aging is the traditional method for producing kusu; the combination of subtropical climate and clay vessel creates a uniquely complex aged spirit.

Kogane Sengan黄金千貫Ko-ga-neh Sen-ganIngredient

The primary sweet potato cultivar used in imo shochu production. A pale-fleshed variety developed in Kagoshima, Kogane Sengan is prized for its high starch content and the archetypal imo shochu flavor it produces: lush sweetness, earthy depth, and the characteristic "imo" aroma that defines the category. Grown extensively in Kagoshima and Miyazaki under the volcanic soil conditions that most favor its development.

Koji麹Ko-jiProduction

Aspergillus mold cultivated on steamed grain to produce enzymes that convert starch to fermentable sugar. The enzymatic foundation of shochu production. Three main strains are used: black koji (kuro koji, Aspergillus luchuensis), white koji (shiro koji, Aspergillus kawachii), and yellow koji (ki koji, Aspergillus oryzae). Each produces a different flavor profile and has different fermentation management requirements.

Koji-muro麹室Ko-ji mu-roProduction

The warm, humid room in which koji is cultivated. Maintained at 28–32°C with 80–90% humidity for the 40–48 hour koji cultivation period. The koji-muro is considered the heart of a distillery — its conditions, microbiome, and management protocols are closely guarded and are a major determinant of house style.

Kokuto黒糖Ko-ku-toType

Brown sugar — the base ingredient of kokuto shochu, produced exclusively in the Amami Islands. "Kokuto" literally means "black sugar." The brown sugar used in kokuto shochu production is produced from sugar cane grown in the Amami Islands and has a distinctive molasses character. Rice koji must be used alongside the kokuto to satisfy Japan's Liquor Tax Act requirements.

Kome米Ko-mehType

Rice — the base ingredient of kome shochu. GI Kuma (Kumamoto Prefecture) is the most prestigious kome shochu region, with 27 distilleries producing some of Japan's most delicate rice spirits. Kome shochu is floral and silky, often compared to sake in aromatic profile but with the clean finish of a distilled spirit. Yellow koji is increasingly used with kome for maximum aromatic expression.

Konwa Shochu混和焼酎Konwa shochuClassification

Blended shochu — a legally defined mixture of honkaku shochu (otsu-rui) and korui shochu (ko-rui). Must contain at least 1% honkaku shochu. Blends containing 50% or more honkaku may carry "honkaku-shu blend" labeling. Common in commercial canned beverages and mid-range izakaya shochu menus.

Korui Shochu甲類焼酎Ko-rui shochuClassification

Literally "type A shochu." Continuously distilled shochu, producing a near-neutral alcohol similar to vodka. Bottled at 36% ABV or below. Unlike honkaku shochu (otsu-rui), korui removes nearly all congeners through multiple distillation passes. Primarily used as a mixer base for chuhai cocktails and blended products. Not consumed as a craft spirit.

Kuro Koji黒麹Production

Black koji — see "Black koji." The original and most traditional koji strain used in shochu and awamori production. Mandatory for all GI Ryukyu awamori. Produces bold, complex spirits with characteristic citric edge.

Kusu古酒Ku-suProduction

Aged awamori — awamori aged for a minimum of 3 years. Under Okinawa Prefecture regulations, at least 51% of the blend must be 3-year-aged awamori to carry the "kusu" designation. Traditional kusu is aged in clay kame pots under Okinawa's subtropical climate. Among the most complex and celebrated spirits produced in Japan, with flavors of caramel, dried fruit, earth, and umami developing over extended aging.

M
Maewari前割りMa-eh-wa-riServing

Pre-diluted shochu — shochu mixed with water in advance (typically 6:4 ratio) and allowed to rest for 2–7 days before serving. The integration period produces a noticeably rounder, silkier character compared to mixing at the point of serving. A traditional practice used by izakaya professionals and serious home shochu enthusiasts. Best served at room temperature or gently warmed.

Mizumachi水待ちMi-zu-ma-chiProduction

Literally "waiting with water." The resting period after dilution (warimizu) during which the shochu and water molecules integrate before bottling or serving. Like maewari, mizumachi allows the diluted spirit to develop a rounder, more harmonious character. Some producers extend this resting period for weeks or months as part of their production standard.

Mizuwari水割りMi-zu-wa-riServing

Shochu served with cold water — typically at a 5:5 or 6:4 (shochu:water) ratio. The most versatile serving style, suitable for all types of shochu and awamori. Softens the alcohol while preserving aromatic complexity. The standard serving style for awamori in Okinawa and the default choice when drinking unknown or unfamiliar expressions for the first time.

MoromiもろみMo-ro-miProduction

The fully fermented mash used as the basis for distillation in honkaku shochu production. Shochu moromi typically reaches 14–17% alcohol before distillation. The primary moromi (ichi-ji moromi) is established from koji, water, and yeast; the secondary moromi (niji moromi) is created by adding the main base ingredient (sweet potato, barley, etc.) to the primary. The quality and character of the moromi is the primary determinant of the finished spirit's quality.

Mugi麦Mu-giType

Barley — the base ingredient of mugi shochu. Oita Prefecture is the largest mugi shochu producer, often using reduced-pressure distillation for a lighter, more approachable style. GI Iki (Iki Island, Nagasaki) mandates a 2:1 barley-to-rice-koji ratio producing a richer, more aromatic style. Mugi shochu is generally the lightest and most accessible honkaku category, excellent on the rocks or as sodawari.

O
Otsu-rui乙類Ot-su-ru-iClassification

The official Liquor Tax Act classification for honkaku shochu — single-distilled pot still shochu. "Type B" in the Japanese classification system (ko-rui = type A, otsu-rui = type B). All honkaku shochu — imo, mugi, kome, kokuto, soba — is classified as otsu-rui. The legal designation that mandates single distillation and preserves the spirit's characterful connection to its base ingredient.

Oyuwariお湯割りOh-yu-wa-riServing

Shochu mixed with hot water — the most traditional and culturally significant serving style for imo shochu. The classic ratio is 6:4 (shochu:hot water), though 5:5 and 7:3 are also common. The hot water should be 70–80°C (not boiling). Pour hot water first, then add shochu. The warmth unlocks aromatic compounds, transforming the spirit's earthy sweetness into something fragrant and comforting. Best for imo and kokuto shochu; excellent in cold weather.

R
Reduced-pressure distillation減圧蒸留Gennatsu jouryuuProduction

Distillation at reduced atmospheric pressure, lowering the boiling point of the moromi to approximately 45°C. Produces lighter, cleaner, more neutral spirits with fewer heavy congeners. Introduced in the mid-20th century. Dominates commercial mugi shochu production in Oita. Contrasts with atmospheric (jonatsu) distillation, which operates at 85°C and produces bolder, more characterful spirits.

Ryukyu Kingdom琉球王国History

The independent kingdom that governed Okinawa and the surrounding islands from the 15th to 19th centuries. A major maritime trading hub connecting China, Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asia. The trade routes of the Ryukyu Kingdom were responsible for introducing distillation technology and Thai indica rice to Okinawa — the origin of awamori production. The kingdom's dissolution in 1879 (Ryukyu shobun — "disposal of Ryukyu") integrated Okinawa into the Japanese state.

S
Shiro Koji白麹Shi-ro ko-jiProduction

White koji — Aspergillus kawachii. A natural mutation of black koji discovered by mycologist Genichiro Kawachi in the 1920s. Produces less citric acid than black koji, resulting in smoother, more approachable spirits. Now the most widely used koji strain in commercial imo shochu production. Produces rounder, sweeter expressions compared to black koji's bolder, more complex character.

Shitsugi仕次ぎShi-tsu-giProduction

The Okinawan practice of replenishing kusu aging kame pots with younger awamori as aged spirit is removed — functionally equivalent to the Spanish solera system used in sherry and Cognac production. Creates a continuously evolving blend where the younger addition absorbs character from the older remaining spirit. Traditional shitsugi kame can contain fractions of awamori accumulated over generations, representing a living heritage of Okinawan culture.

Single cask shochuシングルカスク焼酎Production

Shochu bottled from a single storage vessel without blending with other batches. Analogous to single cask Scotch whisky. Single cask expressions represent the most transparent view of a specific batch's character — the specific koji lot, sweet potato harvest, fermentation conditions, and storage vessel. Increasingly common among premium artisan producers seeking to demonstrate terroir and vintage variation.

Soba Shochuそば焼酎Type

Honkaku shochu distilled from buckwheat (soba). Invented in 1973 by Unkai Shuzo of Miyazaki Prefecture — the world's first commercial buckwheat shochu. Characterized by a nutty, herbal, toasted grain quality. Miyazaki remains the spiritual home of soba shochu. No GI designation. Naturally gluten-free (buckwheat is not related to wheat). Excellent as mizuwari or sodawari.

Sodawariソーダ割りSoh-da-wa-riServing

Shochu mixed with sparkling water — the shochu highball. Build over ice: pour shochu, then carefully add sparkling water to preserve carbonation. Ratio: typically 4:6 (shochu:soda) to 3:7 for lighter result. Best with mugi and soba shochu, where bubbles carry the nutty aromatics. Can be garnished with citrus. Related to chuhai when commercial korui shochu is used as the base.

T
Tanshiki Jouryuki単式蒸留機Tan-shi-ki jou-ryu-kiProduction

Pot still — the distillation equipment used for honkaku shochu production. Single-pass distillation through a pot still (as opposed to continuous distillation in a column still) is the definitional requirement for honkaku/otsu-rui classification. The pot still preserves congeners from the moromi that give each shochu type its distinctive character.

3M3MCulture

The collective name for three iconic premium Kagoshima imo shochu brands — Mao (魔王, "Demon King"), Sato (佐藤), and Mori Izo (森伊蔵) — that became legendary and nearly impossible to obtain through normal retail channels in the 2000s due to explosive demand. Their limited production quantities and consistently high quality created a collector's market with secondary prices 5–10x retail value. The 3M phenomenon transformed shochu's public image from working-class drink to premium collectible spirit.

Toji杜氏Toh-jiCulture

The master distiller/brewer responsible for the entire production process. The toji makes all key decisions: koji cultivation parameters, fermentation management, distillation cuts, storage vessel selection, and blending ratios. In traditional shochu production, the toji was often a seasonal employee (seasonal workers called toji-dan) who traveled from their home region to the distillery for the production season. Today many distilleries have year-round permanent toji. The role combines technical mastery with deep sensory skill.

W
Warimizu割水Wa-ri-mi-zuProduction

The production process of diluting the fresh distillate (37–44% ABV) with water to reach the final bottling strength (typically 25% ABV). The quality of water used for warimizu is as critical as the fermentation water — soft, low-mineral water is preferred to avoid precipitation and cloudiness. Some producers extend the mizumachi (resting period) after warimizu for weeks or months to allow full integration before bottling.

White koji白麹Production

See "Shiro Koji." A natural mutation of black koji discovered in the 1920s. Produces smoother, more approachable spirits than black koji due to lower citric acid production. The dominant commercial koji strain for imo shochu production. Produces rounder, more approachable character without the tartness associated with black koji.

Y
Yellow koji黄麹Ki kojiProduction

Aspergillus oryzae — the koji strain traditionally used in sake brewing and miso fermentation. Highly aromatic but historically risky in warm climates due to low citric acid production (leaving the moromi vulnerable to spoilage). Modern refrigerated fermentation facilities allow safe use in shochu production, creating the most aromatic, floral, and complex expressions — with jasmine, lychee, and citrus notes. Increasingly popular in premium kome and imo shochu production.

Yunomi湯呑みYu-no-miServing

Traditional ceramic tea cup used for serving oyuwari (hot shochu). Ceramic retains heat far better than glass, keeping the drink warm throughout. The rounded shape concentrates the aromatic steam rising from the hot water. Thick-walled yunomi are preferred for maximum heat retention. The traditional vessel for imo shochu in Kagoshima drinking culture.

Z
Zenryo-koji全量麹Zen-ryo ko-jiProduction

All-koji fermentation — the production method in which 100% of the grain is converted to koji before fermentation begins. Unique to awamori production in the world of commercial distilled spirits. Creates an exceptionally acidic, enzyme-rich fermentation that produces awamori's distinctive complex, earthy character. The defining technical distinction between awamori and all other shochu types, where only a portion of the grain is made into koji.
