Chapter 1 — Shochu Guide

Types of Shochu

Japan's honkaku shochu is defined by its base ingredient — sweet potato, barley, rice, brown sugar, buckwheat, or Okinawan indica rice. Each variety carries the terroir of its region and the personality of its koji. Here is the complete taxonomy.

The Most Iconic Type

Imo Shochu — Sweet Potato (芋焼酎)

When most Japanese people say "shochu," they picture imo (芋) — sweet potato shochu. It is the most produced variety in Japan by volume, the most complex in aroma, and the one that most powerfully expresses regional terroir. Originating in Kagoshima Prefecture (historically Satsuma domain), imo shochu has been made for at least 350 years in the volcanic soils of southern Kyushu, where the mineral-rich ground and warm, humid climate produce some of the world's finest sweet potatoes.

The primary sweet potato variety used is Kogane Sengan (黄金千貫 — "golden thousand"), a pale-fleshed cultivar developed in Kagoshima that delivers the archetypal imo shochu profile: lush, rounded sweetness, earthy depth, vanilla-tinged warmth, and a long, lingering finish. Think roasted sweet potato on a winter evening, with a subtle savory underpinning. This is why oyuwari — shochu mixed with hot water at a 6:4 ratio — is the definitive serving style: heat unlocks the aromatic compounds and transforms imo shochu into something profoundly comforting.

Beyond Kogane Sengan, distillers increasingly explore heirloom and specialty potato varieties. Murasaki Masari (紫優) and other purple-fleshed potatoes yield strikingly floral, grape-like aromas with a magenta hue in the moromi (mash). Joy White (ジョイホワイト) produces a lighter, crisper imo with citrus and white floral notes, bridging the gap between classic imo and mugi styles. Beni Azuma adds stone fruit complexity. Single-variety, single-harvest imo shochus — analogous to single-vineyard wine — are among the most sought-after bottles in the modern premium market.

Kagoshima, with approximately 120 distilleries, is the undisputed capital of imo shochu and holds the GI Satsuma designation. Miyazaki, immediately to the north, produces around 45 distilleries' worth of imo with subtle stylistic differences — often slightly lighter-bodied due to terroir and production choices. The black koji tradition runs deep in Kagoshima, while Miyazaki distillers more frequently experiment with white and yellow koji.

Serving recommendation: Imo shochu is at its best as oyuwari — pour the hot water first (approximately 80°C), then add the shochu, at a 6:4 (shochu:water) ratio. Use a ceramic cup (yunomi) to retain heat. Let the aromatics bloom for 30 seconds before drinking. On summer evenings, try it mizuwari (with cold water, 5:5 to 4:6) to experience its sweeter, more restrained side.
Clean & Versatile

Mugi Shochu — Barley (麦焼酎)

Mugi (barley) shochu is Japan's second most popular honkaku category and the easiest entry point for those new to the style. It lacks the assertive earthiness of imo shochu, instead offering a clean, lightly nutty, subtly toasty character that is approachable straight from the first sip. Oita Prefecture produces the majority of Japan's mugi shochu, often using reduced-pressure (vacuum) distillation — a technique that lowers the boiling point and produces a particularly light, delicate spirit with less of the "shochu" character that newcomers sometimes find challenging.

Oita's dominant style — exemplified by brands like Iichiko and Nikaido — has made mugi shochu synonymous with accessibility. These are spirits designed for rocks, soda, or cocktail use: clean and neutral enough to partner with nearly anything. Yet this understates the diversity within the category.

The most prestigious mugi shochu comes from Iki Island (壱岐) in Nagasaki Prefecture, holder of the GI Iki designation. The GI specification requires a mandated 1:2 ratio of rice koji to barley — a recipe that dates back to the island's sake-and-shochu dual tradition. The rice koji component adds a richness and sweet depth that transforms the spirit from mere barley into something layered and aromatic: toasted malt, rice blossom, light citrus, with a warming finish. Iki shochu is best experienced at room temperature or oyuwari.

Nagasaki also produces mugi shochu on the Goto Islands and in other island communities, each with local stylistic distinctions. Some Kyushu producers — particularly in Kagoshima — make mugi shochu using atmospheric distillation, producing a richer, more robust character more aligned with imo in intensity. The koji choice matters here too: black koji mugi shochu has a tartness and body that contrasts sharply with the smooth white koji styles that dominate commercial production.

Serving recommendation: Mugi shochu excels on the rocks or as sodawari (with sparkling water, a.k.a. shochu highball). Its clean character also makes it excellent in cocktails. For GI Iki expressions, try it straight at room temperature or as oyuwari to appreciate the full depth of the rice koji component.
Delicate & Floral

Kome Shochu — Rice (米焼酎)

Rice shochu occupies an elegant middle ground: lighter and more aromatic than imo, with more intrinsic character than most mugi. Distilled from rice — the same grain used in sake — kome shochu delivers delicate floral aromas, gentle sweetness, and a silky texture that makes it perhaps the most food-friendly shochu type of all. It neither competes with delicate dishes nor overwhelms the palate.

The spiritual home of kome shochu is Kumamoto Prefecture's Kuma region (球磨郷) — the steep river valleys and mountain terraces of the Kuma River basin in southern Kumamoto. This region holds the GI Kuma designation, Japan's first shochu GI (designated 1995 alongside GI Iki and GI Ryukyu). GI Kuma mandates that the rice used must be grown in the Kuma region, and the water used must also come from Kuma sources — a true expression of place. With 27 distilleries in the Kuma basin, the region produces a remarkable diversity of kome styles: from light and almost sake-like expressions to richer, amber-hued aged varieties that recall Cognac.

Beyond Kuma, Oita Prefecture also produces excellent kome shochu, and a handful of distilleries across Kyushu offer rice expressions as part of their lineup. Some distillers age kome shochu in oak barrels, creating spirits that rival Western whisky in complexity — with vanilla, caramel, and dried fruit alongside the underlying rice character.

Koji choice profoundly shapes kome shochu. Yellow koji — borrowed from sake production — produces the most aromatic, complex rice shochu, with jasmine, lychee, and honeydew notes. This style is increasingly popular but requires strict temperature control. White koji gives a cleaner, more restrained profile. A small number of producers experiment with black koji in kome, yielding a more assertive, citric character rarely associated with rice spirits.

Serving recommendation: Kome shochu is uniquely versatile. Serve chilled (straight or mizuwari) to highlight floral aromatics, or oyuwari to bring out its gentle sweetness. Aged kome shochu (furutaru-juku) is magnificent neat or with a single ice cube — the oak and rice interplay reveals itself slowly over 10–15 minutes in the glass.
Amami Islands Exclusive

Kokuto Shochu — Brown Sugar (黒糖焼酎)

Kokuto (黒糖 — literally "black sugar") shochu is perhaps Japan's most unusual honkaku category, and certainly its most geographically restricted. It can only be legally produced in the Amami Islands — a subtropical archipelago of five inhabited islands (Amami Oshima, Kikaijima, Tokunoshima, Okinoerabujima, and Yoronjima) that form the northern end of the Ryukyu chain, administratively part of Kagoshima Prefecture but culturally distinct.

The legal basis for this exclusivity is a special provision of Japan's Liquor Tax Act granted when the Amami Islands were returned to Japanese administration from U.S. occupation in 1953. The provision allows Amami distillers to use brown sugar (kokuto) as a base ingredient in what would otherwise be classified as shochu. Critically, the law mandates that rice koji must be used in the production — a requirement that defines the category's flavor profile and differentiates it from rum, which is classified as a "spirit" under a different, higher-tax category.

Kokuto shochu has a profile that genuinely surprises: it is sweeter and more molasses-forward than any other honkaku type, with aromas reminiscent of light rum — brown sugar, caramel, tropical fruit, vanilla — yet it has the structure and depth of a distilled shochu rather than the heavy sweetness of molasses rum. The rice koji contribution adds a subtle umami richness and a floral quality that distinguishes it from Caribbean equivalents. At 25% ABV, it is approachable and versatile.

Amami Oshima hosts the majority of kokuto distilleries, with producers like Nishi Shuzo, Yayoi Shuzo, and Amami Oshima Kaiun Shuzo as well-known names. Many distilleries also produce kokuto shochu aged in oak barrels, creating expressions with significant Cognac-like complexity: dried fruit, warm spice, caramel, and a lingering sweetness that makes them well-suited to after-dinner drinking.

Serving recommendation: Kokuto shochu is exceptional mizuwari (with cold water, 4:6 or 5:5 ratio) — the dilution opens its sweetness without heaviness. Rocks is equally fine. For aged kokuto, consider neat or with a few drops of mineral water. Cocktail use is excellent: try it as a rum substitute in a daiquiri or mojito for a uniquely Japanese twist.
The Youngest Classic

Soba Shochu — Buckwheat (そば焼酎)

Soba (buckwheat) shochu is the youngest of Japan's established honkaku categories, born in 1973 when Unkai Shuzo of Miyazaki Prefecture released "Unkai" — the world's first commercially produced buckwheat shochu. Before that moment, soba existed only as food in Japan; no one had thought to distill it. Unkai's experiment worked spectacularly, and today soba shochu is produced by distilleries across Miyazaki and beyond, though Miyazaki remains the category's spiritual home.

Soba shochu is made from buckwheat (Fagopyrum esculentum) — not a true grain but a seed of a flowering plant related to rhubarb, making it naturally gluten-free. The buckwheat is mashed, combined with koji and water, and fermented before distillation. The resulting spirit has a distinctive nutty, earthy quality — roasted soba tea (sobacha), toasted grain, light earthiness — with a clean, dry finish that is lighter and more herbal than imo but more interesting than most mugi. It pairs extraordinarily well with soba noodles (an obvious but genuinely excellent combination) and with lighter Japanese dishes.

The production volume of soba shochu remains modest compared to imo or mugi, and it lacks a GI designation. However, it has a dedicated following among shochu enthusiasts who appreciate its distinctive profile — unlike any other spirit in the world. Miyazaki's Kirishima mountain range, where much of the buckwheat is grown, imparts a cool-climate mineral quality to the finest expressions.

Serving recommendation: Soba shochu is excellent mizuwari (cold water) or sodawari, where its herbal, nutty character is most apparent and refreshing. It is also outstanding oyuwari, where warmth brings out the roasted grain quality. Avoid over-icing, as cold suppresses the buckwheat aromatics.
Okinawa's Ancient Spirit

Awamori — Okinawa's Distinct Tradition (泡盛)

Awamori (泡盛) is legally classified as honkaku shochu under Japanese law, but it is emphatically its own tradition — 600 years old, rooted in Ryukyuan culture, and technically unique in the world. It is covered in its own dedicated chapter in this guide, but here is the essential overview.

Awamori is produced exclusively in Okinawa Prefecture using three non-negotiable ingredients: Thai indica (long-grain) rice, Okinawan water, and black koji (Aspergillus luchuensis). Unlike all other Japanese shochu, awamori uses an all-koji fermentation method — 100% of the rice is converted to koji before mixing with water and yeast for fermentation. This creates an extraordinarily acidic moromi that inhibits spoilage and produces a distinctive flavor: bold, complex, earthy, with notes of forest floor, dried fruit, and tropical spice.

Awamori aged for three or more years is called kusu (古酒 — "aged liquor"), and it is the pinnacle of the category. Traditional kusu is aged in clay pots (shisa-topped kame) under the subtropical Okinawan climate, developing over years into something of extraordinary depth — caramel, dried fig, umami, and an almost cognac-like warmth, yet entirely its own. GI Ryukyu protects awamori under WTO law.

The Foundation of Flavor

Koji: Black, White, and Yellow

All honkaku shochu and awamori depend on koji (麹) — Aspergillus mold — to enzymatically convert grain starches into fermentable sugars. The choice of koji strain is the master distiller's most fundamental decision, determining the fermentation chemistry, spoilage resistance, and ultimately the flavor of the finished spirit. Three strains dominate:

Black Koji
Aspergillus luchuensis / 黒麹
The original koji of Okinawa, historically used across Kyushu before the 1920s. Produces abundant citric acid during fermentation, which strongly inhibits competing bacteria — critical in the hot, humid Kyushu climate before refrigeration. Creates bold, complex spirits with a characteristic tartness and depth.
Character: Bold / Citric / Complex / Traditional
White Koji
Aspergillus kawachii / 白麹
A natural mutant of black koji discovered in the 1920s by Genichiro Kawachi, Kagoshima's mycology pioneer. Produces less citric acid, resulting in a smoother, more approachable fermentation and a rounder, sweeter spirit. Now the most widely used koji in commercial imo shochu production.
Character: Smooth / Round / Approachable / Modern Standard
Yellow Koji
Aspergillus oryzae / 黄麹
The traditional koji of sake brewing — highly aromatic but historically risky in warm climates due to low acid production. Modern refrigerated fermentation facilities allow its use in shochu, creating the most aromatic, floral, and complex expressions. Produces jasmine, lychee, and citrus notes.
Character: Aromatic / Floral / Complex / Sake-like
