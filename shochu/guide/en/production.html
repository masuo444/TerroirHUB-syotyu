Chapter 2 — Shochu Guide

How Shochu Is Made

From koji spore to ceramic jar: the 8 steps that transform sweet potato, barley, or rice into one of the world's most distinctive distilled spirits. Koji science, moromi fermentation, pot still distillation, and aging — fully explained.

Overview

Honkaku vs. Korui: Two Fundamentally Different Spirits

Before exploring production, it is essential to understand that Japan's Liquor Tax Act defines two fundamentally different categories of shochu. Honkaku shochu (本格焼酎), also classified as otsu-rui (乙類 — "type B"), is single-distilled in a pot still from a fermented mash of a single base ingredient. This preserves the natural aromatic and flavor compounds produced during fermentation, giving each variety its distinctive character — the earthy sweetness of imo, the clean nuttiness of mugi, the delicate florality of kome. This is the tradition that defines Japanese craft shochu culture.

Korui shochu (甲類 — "type A") is produced in a continuous still, distilled repeatedly until nearly all congeners are removed, producing a near-neutral alcohol similar to vodka. Bottled at 36% ABV or below, it is primarily used as a base for chuhai cocktails or blended products. This guide focuses entirely on honkaku production.

The Complete Process

8 Steps from Ingredient to Bottle

Traditional honkaku shochu production takes 40–90 days from koji cultivation to bottling — a disciplined craft process that has remained essentially unchanged for 400 years, even as modern facilities have improved hygiene and temperature control.

1
Foundation

Koji Cultivation (製麹 — Seikiku)

Every batch of honkaku shochu begins not with the base ingredient — sweet potato or barley — but with koji. Steamed rice (or, for mugi shochu, a portion of steamed barley) is spread in a warm, humid room called the koji-muro (麹室), maintained at 28–32°C with 80–90% humidity. Koji spores — black, white, or yellow Aspergillus — are sown by hand over the grain and tended continuously over 40–48 hours, during which the toji (master distiller) turns and breaks up the grain multiple times to ensure even penetration of the mold mycelium.

As koji grows, it secretes amylase enzymes that break down starch molecules into fermentable sugars. It also produces citric acid (black/white koji), glucoamylase, and protease — enzymes that will shape the flavor of the finished spirit. Completed koji has a characteristic sweet, chestnut-like aroma and a white or grayish bloom of mold on the surface.

Koji quality is the single most important determinant of flavor. A toji with impeccable koji cultivation skills can produce exceptional shochu from ordinary ingredients; exceptional ingredients cannot compensate for poor koji.
2
Primary Fermentation

First Moromi (一次仕込み — Ichiji Shikomi)

The completed koji is combined with water and shochu yeast starter in a fermentation tank or traditional ceramic jar (kakutsubo). The volume ratio is carefully calculated — typically 1 part koji to 1.2–1.5 parts water. Shochu yeast (specific Saccharomyces cerevisiae strains maintained by the Japan Brewing Association and individual distilleries) is added to begin fermentation.

Over 5–7 days, yeast consumes the sugars released by koji enzymes, producing ethanol and CO2. The mash — the "moto" or "ichi-ji moromi" — reaches approximately 8–12% alcohol. Simultaneously, citric acid produced by the koji acidifies the mash to pH 3.5–4.0, inhibiting competing bacteria and lactic acid spoilage organisms. This natural self-preservation is why black koji historically dominated Kyushu production before refrigeration.

Yellow koji moromi is most vulnerable during this stage — it produces little protective acid, requiring strict temperature control below 15°C. This explains why yellow koji shochu requires modern refrigerated facilities.
3
Adding the Main Ingredient

Second Mash (二次仕込み — Niji Shikomi)

When the primary fermentation is established and robust, the main base ingredient is added: steamed sweet potato, crushed malted barley, steamed rice, dissolved brown sugar, or cooked buckwheat. This creates the niji moromi (二次もろみ) — secondary mash. For imo shochu, timing is critical: sweet potatoes must be processed and added within 24 hours of harvest, as they deteriorate rapidly. Distilleries in Kagoshima receive daily potato deliveries during harvest season (September–January).

Secondary fermentation lasts 10–20 days. The combined action of koji enzymes (saccharification) and yeast (fermentation) converts the base ingredient into alcohol. The moromi may reach temperatures of 25–30°C during peak fermentation, generating significant CO2 and filling the distillery with the distinctive aromas of the base ingredient combined with fermentation character.

4
Completion

Moromi Completion (もろみ完成)

When fermentation is complete — indicated by declining CO2 activity, stable gravity readings, and the toji's sensory assessment — the moromi typically contains 14–17% alcohol. For imo shochu, the moromi has a thick, pungent, intensely sweet-potato character and a slightly sour edge from the citric acid. The toji samples the moromi repeatedly throughout fermentation.

The quality of the moromi at completion is the highest predictor of the final spirit's quality. Distillation cannot repair a flawed moromi; it can only concentrate what is there — good and bad alike. A skilled toji can identify off-notes that will be amplified by distillation and will make adjustments accordingly.

5
The Core Process

Pot Still Distillation (単式蒸留 — Tanshiki Jouryuu)

Honkaku shochu is single-distilled in a pot still — the definitional requirement that separates it from korui. The moromi is loaded into the still and heated — by direct flame (in some traditional artisan setups), steam injection, or electric immersion heaters. As the mash heats, volatile compounds vaporize, rise into the neck of the still, travel through the lyne arm, and are condensed into liquid by a water-cooled condenser.

The critical variable is distillation pressure — see the comparison section below. The spirit emerges at approximately 37–44% ABV, significantly above the final bottling strength of 25%, as it will later be diluted. A single distillation pass concentrates alcohol roughly 2–3x above the moromi's starting strength while simultaneously concentrating all the aromatic compounds that define the spirit's character.

Some premium producers use a double-kettle (futago gama) still arrangement — where the first distillation produces a rough spirit that is then redistilled for greater refinement. This practice is traditional in some parts of Okinawa for high-grade awamori production.
6
The Art of Cutting

Heads, Heart, and Tails (前留・中取り・後留)

As the still begins to produce distillate, the toji monitors closely. The heads (前留, maedori) — the first portion — contain methanol, acetaldehyde, and volatile sulfur compounds that create harsh, unpleasant notes. These are discarded entirely. The heart (中取り, nakatori) is then collected — this represents the bulk of the desirable distillate and contains the best balance of esters, aldehydes, and alcohols that constitute the spirit's flavor identity.

The final portion — the tails or feints (後留, atodori) — contains heavier fusel oils and less desirable heavy compounds and is separated. The point at which the toji cuts from head to heart, and from heart to tail, is determined by experience, sensory assessment of the flowing distillate, and measurement of alcohol content. These cuts are among the most skill-dependent decisions in shochu production. A tight cut produces a purer, more elegant spirit; a wider cut gives more body and rustic complexity.

Heads and tails are not always discarded. Some distilleries redistill them, or blend small amounts of tails back into later batches to add body and depth to commercial expressions.
7
Time and Transformation

Storage and Maturation (貯蔵 — Chozou)

Freshly distilled shochu is often raw and harsh — like new make spirit straight from the still. A minimum maturation period (typically 3–6 months, sometimes 1–3 years) is required before release. During this time, volatile sulfur compounds dissipate, rough edges integrate, and the spirit develops depth and coherence. The storage vessel chosen profoundly shapes the final character.

Stainless steel tanks produce clean, neutral aging that preserves the pure spirit character. Ceramic kakutsubo jars — the traditional choice — impart subtle minerality from the unglazed clay and allow slow micro-oxygenation through the vessel walls, softening the spirit without adding flavor. Oak barrels introduce vanilla, caramel, spice, and amber color, producing whisky-adjacent expressions. Awamori kusu is traditionally stored in large clay kame for three or more years, developing extraordinary complexity under Okinawa's subtropical climate.

8
Final Step

Dilution and Bottling (割水・瓶詰め)

The mature distillate (37–44% ABV) is diluted with pure, soft mineral water to the desired final strength — typically 25% ABV. This dilution, called warimizu (割水), is itself a craft process: water must be chemically compatible with the spirit (soft water with low mineral content prevents precipitation), and dilution must be gradual and well-mixed. Many distilleries prize their local spring or groundwater for dilution, considering it as important as fermentation water.

Genshi (原酒 — undiluted) expressions are bottled at cask strength (37–44% ABV) without dilution, offering powerful, intensely flavored shochu that rewards patient appreciation. Genshi expressions are increasingly popular among connoisseurs seeking full-throttle expression of the base ingredient and koji character. Light filtration may be applied to remove haze, then the spirit is bottled — traditionally in painted ceramic tokkuri for premium expressions, though most production uses glass bottles.

Some producers practice "maewari" (前割り) — pre-diluting shochu with water days before serving or bottling, then allowing the blend to rest. This integration period produces a noticeably rounder, more harmonious spirit compared to dilution at the point of drinking.
Technical Deep Dive

Atmospheric vs. Reduced-Pressure Distillation

The choice of distillation pressure is one of the most consequential production decisions a shochu distillery makes, as it fundamentally determines the flavor profile of the finished spirit.

Atmospheric Distillation
常圧蒸留 — Jonatsu Jouryuu
Operates at normal atmospheric pressure. The moromi boils at approximately 85°C. This higher temperature volatilizes a broad spectrum of aromatic compounds — including heavier esters, fusel alcohols, and the characteristic aroma molecules of the base ingredient. Produces the full-bodied, richly aromatic spirits associated with traditional imo shochu. Considered by artisan producers to best express terroir and base ingredient character.
Boiling point~85°C
CharacterBold / Rich / Aromatic
Best forImo / GI Iki Mugi / Premium

Reduced-Pressure Distillation
減圧蒸留 — Gennatsu Jouryuu
Lowers atmospheric pressure inside the still, reducing the boiling point of the moromi to approximately 45°C. At this lower temperature, lighter, more volatile aromatic compounds predominate, while heavier congeners remain in the still. The result is a lighter, cleaner, more neutral spirit — approachable, mixer-friendly, and consistent. Dominates commercial mugi shochu production in Oita and large-volume operations.

CharacterLight / Clean / Approachable
Best forCommercial Mugi / Entry-level

Some distilleries blend distillate from both methods to achieve a desired flavor balance — using atmospheric distillation for depth and body, reduced-pressure for cleanliness and accessibility. This blending approach is a legitimate and skillful craft technique common among major producers.
